## The Insight Formula

```
[Observation about behavior] + [Underlying motivation] = Insight

"Moms search parenting advice at 2am"
+ "They want validation they're not alone, not just answers"
= "Moms don't need more information. They need to know they're not the only ones awake wondering."
```

Use this when the user is stuck generating facts instead of insights. The formula forces a connection between observable behavior and emotional motivation — that connection is where insights live.

## The Strategy Bridge

```
WHERE WE ARE          ->          WHERE WE WANT TO BE
[Current State]                   [Desired State]
      |                                 |
   INSIGHT                          ADVANTAGE
      |                                 |
            ---- STRATEGY ----
                    |
               [How we get there]
```

Use this to visualize the strategic journey. It helps the user see that strategy isn't a destination — it's the bridge between the current reality (illuminated by insight) and the desired future (enabled by advantage).

## The "Only We" Test

Ask: "Could a competitor say this?"
- If yes -> too generic, push harder
- If no -> you've found your edge

Apply this test to every strategy statement before finalizing. Generic strategies waste resources because they don't create differentiation. If Competitor X could swap in their logo and the strategy still works, it's not a strategy — it's a platitude.

## The "So What" Ladder

Keep asking "so what?" until you hit emotional truth:

```
Fact: "Our formula has DHA"
So what? -> "It supports brain development"
So what? -> "Kids might learn better"
So what? -> "Parents feel they're giving their child an advantage"
So what? -> "Parents feel less guilty about things they can't control"
<- THAT's the insight territory
```

Use this when the user presents facts or features instead of insights. Most people stop 2-3 "so whats" too early. The emotional truth at the bottom of the ladder is where strategy gets powerful.

## Good vs. Bad Examples

### Problem Statements

| Bad | Good |
|-----|------|
| "We need to increase brand awareness among millennials" | "Young parents trust advice from other parents more than brands, so our expertise never reaches them" |
| "We need a social media strategy" | "Our audience talks about us in spaces we don't participate in, so we're losing control of our own story" |

The bad versions are either vague goals or tactics disguised as problems. The good versions identify a specific tension between what the brand needs and what humans actually do.

### Insights

| Bad | Good |
|-----|------|
| "Moms are busy and want convenience" | "Moms feel guilty for taking shortcuts, so they look for shortcuts they can feel good about" |
| "People want healthy food" | "People don't want to be healthy — they want to feel like the kind of person who makes healthy choices" |

The bad versions are observations anyone could make. The good versions reveal an underlying motivation that reframes the problem and points toward a specific strategic direction.

### Strategy Statements

| Bad | Good |
|-----|------|
| "Be the most trusted brand for young families" | "Turn mom guilt into mom confidence by making our science feel like support, not selling" |
| "Become the market leader in wellness" | "Own the moment when people feel they deserve better by making wellness feel like rebellion, not restriction" |

The bad versions are aspirational goals, not strategies. The good versions connect a specific insight to a specific action — they tell you what to do and (just as importantly) what NOT to do.
