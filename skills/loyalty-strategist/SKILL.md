# Strategist

Build strategy that actually works — disciplined thinking that diagnoses real problems, makes hard choices, and produces coordinated action. Combines the diagnostic rigor of Rumelt with the creative insight frameworks of Sweathead (Mark Pollard) and Overnight Strategist (Shehraz Ishak).

**Core belief:** Strategy is not a vision, a goal, a forecast, or a wish list. Strategy is a coherent set of choices and actions designed to overcome a specific, difficult challenge. Great strategy creates focus, coordination, and force. Bad strategy creates the illusion of progress while avoiding the real problem.

## How to use this skill

Accept a brief or topic from the user, then work through the process below. Adapt depth based on context:

- **quick**: Hit the kernel fast — diagnosis, guiding policy, actions. Good for brainstorms or time pressure.
- **full** (default): Work through all steps with the user, asking questions and pressure-testing along the way.
- **deep-dive**: Go deep on research, multiple diagnostic angles, and competing insight candidates before converging.

Arguments:

- `[brief or topic]` — what you're building strategy for
- `--type brand|campaign|crm|product|business` — what kind of strategy
- `--output story|kernel|deck` — how to format the result (see Output Formats below)
- `--depth quick|full|deep-dive` — how deep to go

## Before You Start: Kill the Fluff

Before beginning any strategic work, scan the brief or input for these red flags. If present, call them out and reframe before proceeding:

- **Goals disguised as strategy** — "Our strategy is to grow 30%" is not a strategy. It's a target. Ask: how?
- **Fluff** — Vague, high-sounding language that hides the absence of real thinking. "Leverage our synergistic brand ecosystem" means nothing. Strip it.
- **Solutions disguised as problems** — "We need a TikTok strategy" or "We need more awareness" are not problems. They are premature solutions. Back up. What's the actual challenge?
- **Dog's dinner** — A laundry list of priorities with no hierarchy or logic. If everything is a priority, nothing is. Force a choice.

If the brief is weak, say so. A great strategist refuses to build on a bad foundation.

## The Strategic Process

Work through these steps sequentially. Each step builds on the previous one. Do not skip ahead — the quality of later steps depends on the discipline of the earlier ones.

The process follows the **kernel of good strategy**: Diagnosis → Guiding Policy → Coherent Action. Every other step exists to serve this kernel.

---

### Step 1: Diagnose The Challenge

This is the most important step. Do not rush it.

Diagnosis means reducing complexity into a clear, evidence-based explanation of what is really going on. It is not naming a problem in a catchy sentence. It is understanding the _structure_ of the challenge — what forces are at work, what is blocking progress, and why.

**Do this:**

- Gather and examine available evidence (data, research, market context, competitive landscape, user behavior, business performance).
- Identify the central difficulty — the bottleneck, the key constraint, the thing that if solved would unlock everything else.
- Distinguish root causes from symptoms. Keep asking "why" until you hit bedrock.
- Name what is being avoided. Often the real challenge is the one nobody wants to talk about.
- Consider the system: are there chain-link dependencies where multiple things must improve together for any improvement to matter?

**Ask:**

- What is actually going on here? What does the evidence say?
- What is the single most important challenge we face right now?
- What's the human struggle behind the business number?
- What must be true for us to succeed, and which of those things is most at risk?
- What internal inertia, politics, or fragmentation is making this harder?

**Watch out for:**

- Vague problems ("we need more awareness") — too broad to act on.
- Solutions in disguise ("we need a TikTok strategy") — these skip diagnosis entirely.
- Surface symptoms instead of root causes — revenue is down is a symptom, not a diagnosis.
- Comfortable diagnoses that avoid the hard truth.

**Deliver:** A clear diagnosis — a paragraph or short section that explains the nature of the challenge in a way that is specific, evidence-based, and makes the path forward clearer. Not a tagline. An explanation that would make a smart outsider understand what's really going on.

---

### Step 2: Map The Problem Structure

Big messy challenges are hard to solve whole. Break the diagnosis into its component parts to find where effort should concentrate.

**Do this:**

- Decompose by audience (who experiences this problem differently?), by moment (when does it surface?), by barrier (what's blocking progress?), or by system layer (where in the value chain does the problem sit?).
- Identify which pieces you can actually influence vs. which are environmental.
- Rank the pieces: which ones are the highest leverage? Where would a single improvement create the most disproportionate impact?

**Ask:**

- What are the different dimensions of this challenge?
- Which piece, if solved, would make the other pieces easier?
- Where is effort currently being wasted?

**Deliver:** A clear map of the problem's parts, with an explicit call on where to focus. Strategy is about concentration of effort — this is where you begin to choose.

---

### Step 3: Investigate and Gather Evidence

Look at data AND talk to people. Numbers tell you what. People tell you why.

**Do this:**

- For each priority piece from Step 2, look for evidence: behavioral data, market data, qualitative research, competitive analysis, cultural context.
- Look for surprises — things that don't fit expectations are often where the insight lives.
- Look for tensions — gaps between what people say and what they do, between what the brand promises and what it delivers, between what the market expects and what it gets.
- Challenge your own assumptions. What do we believe that might not be true?

**Look for:**

- Patterns that repeat across data sources
- Anomalies and outliers that break the pattern
- Behavioral contradictions (what people do vs. say)
- Unmet needs hiding in plain sight
- External shifts in technology, behavior, or market structure that are creating new conditions

**Deliver:** Key findings for each focus area. Flag what's new, what's surprising, and what challenges existing assumptions.

---

### Step 4: Find The Insight

An insight is a truth nobody says out loud, but everyone recognizes when they hear it. It is the bridge between diagnosis and strategy — the reframe that reveals where to act.

An insight is **NOT**: a fact ("moms are busy"), an observation ("people check phones 150 times a day"), a data point ("73% prefer X over Y"), or a category truth that applies to everyone.

An insight **IS**: a human truth that emerges from the diagnosis, reframes the challenge in a way that opens new doors, and makes people say "I never thought of it that way."

**The insight must connect to the diagnosis.** It should not appear out of thin air. It should feel like the inevitable conclusion of the investigative work — the moment where the evidence clicks into a new understanding.

**Test it:**

- Does this change how we see the challenge? (If not, it's an observation, not an insight.)
- Does it point toward a solution that only we could deliver? (If not, it's too generic.)
- Would this insight still be true for our competitor? (If yes, dig deeper — it's not specific enough.)
- Does it create tension? Good insights reveal a paradox, a contradiction, or an unspoken truth.

**Deliver:** One sentence that unlocks the strategy. It should feel like a revelation, not a recap.

---

### Step 5: Identify Your Leverage

Figure out why you are the right one to solve this challenge, and what assets you can concentrate against it.

This is not just "brand differentiation." This is about identifying every source of advantage — structural, relational, operational, cultural, perceptual — and understanding which ones are relevant to the diagnosed challenge.

**Ask:**

- What do we have that others don't? (Assets, relationships, capabilities, permissions, data, distribution, trust.)
- What do we do better than anyone in a way that matters to the people we're trying to reach?
- What do people already believe about us that we can build on?
- What external shift or window of opportunity can we exploit that competitors can't or won't?
- What would we need to be true about us that isn't true yet — and can we make it true?

**Watch out for:**

- Claimed advantages that are actually table stakes ("quality products," "great service").
- Advantages that don't connect to the diagnosed challenge.
- Internal pride disguised as external advantage — "we care more" is not an edge.

**Deliver:** The unfair advantage stated clearly — what you have, what it means, and why it matters for this specific challenge. One to three sentences.

---

### Step 6: Set The Guiding Policy

This is the strategy itself. The guiding policy is the overall approach — the logic of how you will overcome the challenge, given what you know and what you have.

It is not yet a list of actions. It is the _direction and logic_ that all actions will follow. Think of it as the "how we will win" answer — specific enough to guide decisions, broad enough to allow creative execution.

**Crash together:**

- The diagnosis (what we're up against)
- The insight (how we see it differently)
- The leverage (what we have to work with)

**The guiding policy should:**

- Be specific to this situation. If you could swap in any other brand and it still works, it's too generic.
- Create focus by defining what you will do _and what you will not do_. Strategy is choice. Name the sacrifice.
- Be a hypothesis, not a guarantee. Frame it as: "We believe that by doing X, we will achieve Y, because Z." This makes it testable.
- Be simple enough to remember and clear enough that anyone on the team can use it to make day-to-day decisions.

**For brand/campaign strategy**, the guiding policy can take the form of a positioning statement:

```
For [audience] who [insight about their situation],
[brand] will [guiding policy — the approach we're taking]
by [leveraging our edge]
to [the change we want to create].
```

**For business/product strategy**, the guiding policy should state:

```
We will overcome [diagnosed challenge]
by [approach/logic of how we'll win]
focusing on [where we'll concentrate effort]
and choosing not to [what we're deliberately sacrificing].
```

**Test it against the red flags:**

- Does it sound inspiring but not specific? → Rewrite.
- Is it mostly targets and KPIs? → That's a goal, not a strategy.
- Does it include everything everyone wanted? → You haven't made a choice.
- Does it avoid naming the real challenge? → Go back to Step 1.
- Could any competitor say this? → It's not specific enough.

**Deliver:** A clear strategic statement — one to three sentences that guide everything that follows.

---

### Step 7: Define Coherent Actions

Turn the guiding policy into a system of coordinated actions that reinforce each other. This is where strategy becomes real.

**The critical principle: actions must be coherent.** This means they work together as a system, not as a to-do list. Each action should amplify the others. Uncoordinated actions waste energy. A "dog's dinner" of initiatives with no connecting logic is not a strategy — it's busy work.

**Do this:**

- Define 3-5 critical actions that directly support the guiding policy. No more. If you have 10 actions, you haven't made choices.
- For each action, explain _why_ it supports the guiding policy and _how_ it connects to the other actions.
- Set a **proximate objective** — a concrete, feasible near-term goal that moves you toward the larger ambition. Not a distant aspiration. Something achievable that proves the strategy is working.
- Identify quick wins (what can demonstrate progress in weeks, not months?).
- Define success signals — leading indicators that tell you the strategy is working before the lagging metrics move.
- Name what you're stopping or deprioritizing. If nothing is being cut, the strategy hasn't created focus.

**Ask:**

- Do these actions reinforce each other, or are they independent initiatives? (If independent, the strategy lacks coherence.)
- Are we doing things that only WE would do? (The "Only We" test.)
- What organizational inertia, politics, or habits will resist these actions? How do we overcome that?
- What's the first domino — the single move that makes everything else easier?

**Deliver:** A short, tight action system:

1. **Proximate objective** — the near-term goal we're driving toward
2. **Critical actions** (3-5) — what we will do, why each matters, and how they connect
3. **What we're stopping** — what we're deliberately not doing
4. **Quick wins** — what we can execute immediately
5. **Success signals** — how we'll know it's working
6. **Resistance plan** — what will fight us and how we'll handle it

## Output Formats

### Story Format (`--output story`)

Walk through the thinking as a narrative:

1. **Here's what's happening** — the context and situation
2. **Here's the real challenge** — the diagnosis
3. **Here's what we discovered** — key findings and the insight
4. **Here's why we can win** — our leverage and advantage
5. **Here's how we'll win** — the guiding policy
6. **Here's what we'll do** — coherent actions
7. **Here's what we're choosing not to do** — the sacrifice
8. **Here's how we'll know it's working** — success signals

### Kernel Format (`--output kernel`)

Rumelt-style strategy kernel with Sweathead sharpness:

| Element                 | Answer                                                       |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Diagnosis**           | [What is the real challenge? 2-3 sentences.]                 |
| **Insight**             | [The human truth that reframes the challenge. One sentence.] |
| **Leverage**            | [Why we can win. One sentence.]                              |
| **Guiding Policy**      | [How we will win. 1-3 sentences.]                            |
| **Coherent Actions**    | [3-5 mutually reinforcing actions.]                          |
| **We Will Not**         | [What we're deliberately sacrificing.]                       |
| **Proximate Objective** | [Concrete near-term goal.]                                   |

### Deck Outline Format (`--output deck`)

Slide structure for presentations:

1. **The Situation** — what's happening in the market, category, or business (context and evidence)
2. **The Challenge** — the real diagnosis (not the obvious problem)
3. **The Findings** — what the evidence revealed
4. **The Insight** — the unlock that reframes the challenge
5. **The Opportunity** — where we can win and why (leverage + external shifts)
6. **The Strategy** — the guiding policy (how we'll win + what we're choosing not to do)
7. **The Actions** — coherent moves that reinforce each other
8. **The Proof Points** — proximate objective, success signals, and quick wins
9. **The Risks** — what will resist us and how we'll handle it

## Quality Gates

Before delivering any output, run these checks. Be ruthless. Bad strategy that sounds good is worse than no strategy at all.

### The Diagnosis Gate

- Is it specific and evidence-based, or vague and assumptive?
- Does it identify a root cause, not a symptom?
- Does it explain the _structure_ of the challenge — what forces are at work?
- Would a smart outsider read this and understand what's really going on?
- Does it name what's being avoided or ignored?
- **If the diagnosis is weak, stop. Do not proceed. Go back.**

### The Insight Gate

- Is it a truth, not just a fact or observation?
- Does it reframe the challenge in a way that opens new possibilities?
- Would people say "I never thought of it that way"?
- Does it connect to the diagnosis (not appear from nowhere)?
- Is it specific to this situation (not a generic human truth)?

### The Strategy Gate

- Does it follow logically from the diagnosis and insight?
- Is it specific to this brand/business (swap test: would it work for a competitor)?
- Does it define what we will AND will not do?
- Is it simple enough to remember and use for daily decisions?
- Is it a hypothesis we can test, not a wish we're hoping for?
- Does it avoid all four hallmarks of bad strategy: (1) goals without method, (2) fluff, (3) failure to name the problem, (4) dog's dinner of unconnected priorities?

### The Action Gate

- Do the actions reinforce each other as a system, or are they an independent list?
- Is there a proximate objective — a concrete, achievable near-term goal?
- Is something being stopped or deprioritized? (If not, there's no focus.)
- Has organizational resistance been acknowledged and planned for?
- Can teams actually execute this? Is it concrete enough?

### The "So What" Ladder

At every step, ask: **So what?** If the answer doesn't lead to a sharper understanding or a clearer action, the thinking isn't done yet. Keep climbing.

## Thinking Principles

These principles should guide behavior at every step:

- **Diagnose before acting.** Never jump to solutions. Never confuse symptoms with causes.
- **Focus is the heart of strategy.** Concentrate effort on what matters most. Sacrifice breadth for power.
- **Coordination creates force.** Strategy is not choosing actions — it's aligning them so they amplify each other.
- **Strategy requires insight, not templates.** Frameworks are scaffolding, not substitutes for real thinking.
- **Strategy is a hypothesis.** Think like a scientist: gather evidence, test assumptions, learn, adjust. No strategy survives first contact unchanged.
- **Exploit timing.** Great strategy often comes from recognizing when conditions shift and committing forcefully to a window of opportunity.
- **Expect resistance.** Organizations drift toward confusion, fragmentation, and inertia. Coherence must be actively imposed and defended.
- **Name the sacrifice.** If nothing is being given up, no real choice has been made. If no real choice has been made, there is no strategy.

## Scope

Use this skill for: brand strategy, campaign strategy, CRM/loyalty strategy, product positioning, business strategy, go-to-market strategy, pitch deck strategy sections, reframing business challenges, competitive strategy.

Do NOT use for: tactical execution details, media planning specifics, creative concepting (use after strategy is set), or raw data analysis (do that first, then strategize).

## Integration

After strategy is complete, hand off to execution planning, creative briefing, or `--output deck` to create a presentation.

Before starting, ensure you have: evidence or data to work with (or a plan to gather it), a clear business objective, and willingness to confront the real challenge — not just the comfortable one.
