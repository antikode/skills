# Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) Framework

Reference guide for analyzing user motivations and journey gaps in website audits.

---

## Overview

Jobs to Be Done is a customer-centric framework that reframes user journeys around the *job* users are trying to accomplish, rather than demographic segments or product features.

Instead of: "Our users are 25–35, tech-savvy professionals"  
Think: "Users are trying to evaluate if this service solves their specific problem faster/cheaper than alternatives"

---

## Core Concepts

### The Job, Not the Person

A **job** is the progress a customer wants to make in a particular circumstance.

**Jobs have:**
- A **functional** dimension (what do they need to do?)
- An **emotional** dimension (how do they want to feel?)
- A **social** dimension (how do they want to be perceived?)

### Example: Indonesian Automotive Site

**Functional Job:** "Evaluate whether Brand X trucks meet our fleet's payload and fuel efficiency requirements"

**Emotional Job:** "Feel confident I'm making a smart investment for the business"

**Social Job:** "Be seen as a decisive leader who chose a reliable, trusted brand"

Without understanding all three, the site might fail to address emotional or social hesitations even if the functional specs are comprehensive.

## The Job Map (Four Stages)

### 1. **Trigger / Situation**
What circumstances cause the user to seek a solution?

**Questions to Ask:**
- What's the problem or challenge they're facing?
- What's forcing the decision now (time pressure, budget cycle, business growth)?
- Are they comparing alternatives or newly shopping?

**Example (Indonesian Bank):**
- Trigger: "SME owner needs working capital before end of quarter"
- Not a routine refinance; a *moment of urgency*

### 2. **Search & Learning**
How do they learn about options and evaluate them?

**Questions to Ask:**
- Where do they start searching? (Google, LinkedIn, WhatsApp, friends, industry forums?)
- What information do they need to *compare* options fairly?
- Are they looking for reassurance or detailed specs?
- Do competitors appear in their search?

**Example:**
- SME owner searches "quick business loan Indonesia" → lands on bank homepage
- Needs: approval timeline, interest rate, required documents, past customer testimonials
- If website lacks *any* of these, they bounce to competitor

### 3. **Selection & Decision**
What factors drive the final choice?

**Questions to Ask:**
- What's the *minimum* information needed to decide?
- Are they risk-averse (want case studies, testimonials, guarantees)?
- Do they need human reassurance (call a relationship manager) or do they self-serve?
- What's the *last* barrier before they commit?

**Example:**
- SME wants to call a loan officer to confirm eligibility → no phone number prominent on the site
- Or: Wants to see past success stories → testimonials buried in footer
- Or: Worried about hidden fees → no transparent pricing table

### 4. **Usage & Advocacy**
How do they *use* the service, and would they recommend it?

**Questions to Ask:**
- Post-purchase, what support do they need?
- Are there onboarding gaps (confusion during setup)?
- Do they feel good about the decision afterward?
- Would they recommend to peers? If not, why not?

**Example:**
- Loan approved, but onboarding process is slow/unclear
- → Regret, even though the product itself was good
- → Won't recommend; damages word-of-mouth (critical in Indonesian market)

## How to Apply JTBD to Web Audits

### Step 1: Define the Job(s)

Before analyzing the site, articulate **what job the site needs to help users accomplish**.

For each user segment, answer:

**Functional:** What task/goal?  
**Emotional:** How should they feel?  
**Social:** What persona do they want?

Example Matrix:

| User Segment | Functional Job | Emotional Job | Social Job |
|---|---|---|---|
| **SME Loan Shopper** | Get working capital approval in <7 days | Feel confident the decision won't backfire | Be seen as a savvy operator who found a good deal |
| **Enterprise Fleet Manager** | Compare truck models on payload + fuel efficiency | Feel protected against long-term reliability risks | Be trusted by peers as a thorough decision-maker |
| **Retail Customer** | Purchase product + arrange delivery | Feel like I got a good deal | Impress friends with my smart purchase |

### Step 2: Map Current Site Journey Against the Job

Does the site *enable* users to make progress on each dimension?

**Functional Enablers:**
- Is the information needed *present* and *easy to find*?
- Can users compare options side-by-side?
- Is the process frictionless (forms, CTAs, checkouts)?

**Emotional Enablers:**
- Does the copy + design reassure or create doubt?
- Are there social proof elements (testimonials, case studies, awards)?
- Is there a human contact option for reassurance?

**Social Enablers:**
- Does the brand positioning align with how users want to be perceived?
- Are there shareable moments (easy to save, email, or forward)?
- Is credibility/authority clear?

### Step 3: Identify Struggle Moments (Journey Gaps)

Where does the site *fail* to support the job?

**Common Struggle Moments:**

- **Information gaps** — Users can't find specs/pricing/reviews → bounce to competitor
- **Emotional friction** — Copy is corporate/cold, no reassurance → doubt creeps in
- **Comparison barriers** — Can't compare multiple options → unclear decision
- **Human gap** — Need a person to talk to, but can't find contact info → abandon
- **Post-decision regret** — Lack of post-purchase support → buyer's remorse

## Indonesian Market JTBD Considerations

### Trust & Relationship
- Indonesian buying culture emphasizes personal relationships
- **Job:** "Feel I can trust this brand and the person representing it"
- **Solution:** Prominent "Meet the Team," "Our Story," WhatsApp contact, video testimonials from local customers

### Word-of-Mouth
- Peer recommendations are the #1 driver
- **Job:** "Feel confident enough to recommend this to my network"
- **Solution:** Easy share buttons, customer success stories, referral incentives

### Mobile-First & Social Commerce
- Shopping happens on WhatsApp, Instagram, TikTok, not just websites
- **Job:** "Get answers and complete transaction on my phone in 5 minutes"
- **Solution:** Mobile-optimized site, WhatsApp integration, instant checkout

### Speed & Urgency
- Quick decision-making preferred (not lengthy deliberation)
- **Job:** "Make a decision and move forward without overthinking"
- **Solution:** Clear CTAs, approval timelines, success stories showing fast results

### Value & Price Sensitivity
- Price is often the deciding factor
- **Job:** "Be absolutely certain I'm getting the best value for money"
- **Solution:** Transparent pricing, competitor comparison, ROI calculator

## How JTBD Complements Nielsen's Heuristics

| Framework | Focuses On | In This Audit |
|---|---|---|
| **Nielsen's 10 Heuristics** | Usability problems (UI/UX friction) | Section 6 (Accessibility & UX) |
| **JTBD** | Motivational problems (does it help me achieve my goal?) | Section 8 (User Journey Gaps) |

**Together they answer:**
- Nielsen: "Is the interface easy to use?"
- JTBD: "Does the interface help me accomplish what I came to do?"

A site can be *usable* but not *useful* — that's where JTBD catches the gap.
